Enhancing the offers experience at Victoria's Secret

Led design efforts for improving discoverability and usability of offers across desktop and mobile website

  • Goal: To enable users to discover, understand and redeem offers available on the website
  • What I did: User journey map, sketching, prototyping, card sorting, usability testing, A/B testing and analyzing analytics

Offers were one of most loved features of the shopping experience for a Victoria's Secret customer. But they were also one of the top issues that customers had problem with. Customers had a hard time finding offers on the website, as indicated by the call volume to the customer care center. Further, customers had a hard time understanding offers and applying them. The main goal of this project was to improve the offers experience for customer on all platforms (mobile, tablet and web).


Difficult to discover

The only way to trigger the offer drawer on desktop was to click Today's Offers. This trigger was missed by a lot of customers, as seen in various research studies, mainly because it was not prominent enough.


A potential to improve usability

On opening the drawer, the customer would be presented with a scrollable row of offer cards, often exceeding 20 offers. A customer who would miss out on the carets in the drawer would wrongly assume that the website only offered 4 offers.


A costly diversion

Offers on the mobile website were present on a dedicated Offers page. To view these offers, a customer had to navigate away from the page they were viewing. This diversion caused customers to abandon their shopping journey, potentially leading to a loss in revenue. Moreover, the list of offers on this page meant that a user had to scroll quite a bit to view all offers. This did not provide any value to users looking for brand-specific or product-specific offers.



APPROACHING THE REDESIGN PROCESS

I planned the redesign of the Offers drawer using a multi-phase approach, where each phase aimed to achieve a specific outcome. This could be also referred to as the incremental correctness approach. The work was prioritized based on the level of effort required and the impact it would create. I planned to tackle low effort, high impact work first, working towards approaching an ideal state.

Improving discoverability

The first step was to make the Offers drawer trigger more prominent. I had been working on a different project which involved making the sitewide offer (Free Shipping offer) more flexible. Based on the requirements, I had decided to move the offer to its own space above the global navigation. So I repurposed that space to also include the trigger for the Offers drawer. This space could potentially be used for showing multiple offers, personalized offers and contextual offers as well.


Improving usability

I decided to reuse an existing design pattern and retrofit the offers inside it thus creating a vertical Offers drawer. This change would allow not only allow a more natural way to scroll offers but also open up opportunities to improve the contents of the drawer. This vertical drawer would make the offers experience consistent and bring parity across all devices (desktop, tablet, mobile).


Making offers easy to understand

The way we presented offers to the customers had potential for some improvements. The hypothesis was that grouping offers would reduce extraneous cognitive load on customers, given that studies show humans have a limited working memory and that time to make a decision increases exponentially with the number of choices available. An optimal grouping strategy would also enable customers to easily discover offers suited to their needs. These benefits would in turn increase offer redemption and improve customer loyalty.

To solve this issue, I decided to create brand-specific tabs. Not only would this create a logical segmentation of offers but also it could allow each brand to directly link customers to their own section inside the Offers drawer. Since each offer was already set up in the CMS with the brand it referred to, it required a very small effort on the front-end side to create the tab functionality.

TAKING A PAUSE

At this point, it was necessary to test our hypotheses and observe how customers would engage with the functionality we had developed so far. This step was crucial in making sure we had covered our bases, and most importantly, discovering areas where we could improve the design. I developed click through protoypes for the desktop and mobile experience, and conducted moderated usability testing with a total of 12 participants. The usability test involved a couple of tasks with a pre and post test survey.

CONTINUING THE MOMENTUM

As I updated the container for the offers, another issue became apparent. Historically, sitewide offers were always prioritized over brand-specific offers. Since they were applicable to the entire website, they were visible under each brand-specific tab. So, if there were a couple of sitewide offers available, a customer switching between the brand tabs in the drawer would see the same few offers above the fold of the page. This could potentially lead to confusion at the customer's end. There was a potential to make the offer card design consistent and compact which would alleviate this issue while improving the customer experience.

Studying the offer cards

Even though all offer cards were set up in CMS, their structure varied across brands and offer types. Due to lack of standards and the need for more flexibility, the brands used a variety of hacks to customize the look of their offer cards. Inconsistent designs made it slightly harder for a customer to understand the offers at a glance. There was a clear opportunity to make the offer cards consistent, with a well defined information hierarchy. A simplified offer card structure would also allow more offer cards to be visible in the drawer, at any given time.


BUT WHAT CONSTITUTES AN IDEAL OFFER?

To understand what constitutes an ideal offer card, I decided to set up a card sorting exercise. The goal of this exercise was to understand which components of an offer card were important to the customer, why they were important and how the delivery mechanism (web, mobile, email) influenced the ideal design. This exercise was done remotely over Figma, with participants dragging pieces of information into place based on their preferences. There were 4 themes to this exercise â€“ lingerie, beauty, accessories and apparel. I chose to use generic examples to prevent customers' biases/ preferences from affecting their decisions/ true choices.


CARD SORT STUDY INSIGHTS

  • According to the test participants, the most important information in an offer card is - what is the offer, how do I redeem it and how long do I have to redeem it (Title, Offer Code, Label)
  • A tangible deadline is more compelling and invokes a sense of urgency (Ends Friday > Limited Time Offer)
  • Images convey information about the product and fit but are not important in an offer
  • Offer code was deemed to be relatively more important than Click to Apply action
  • Shop Now provides a convenient way of redeeming an offer but is not an essential component in the offer card
  • Desired information density is dependent on the medium of offer delivery (website/ mobile/ email)

DESIGN EXPLORATIONS

I converted the actionable insights from the card sort exercise into tangible outputs. My focus was to design an offer card structure with a clear hierarchy of information based on customer needs, that could be used on mobile as well as the desktop website. I also explored a few high fidelity options to ensure that I captured brand-specific use cases and that a single design structure could work for all 4 brands.

PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

Here is what an ideal state would look like with all of the aforementioned improvements in place. This step was necessary to ensure that all design decisions still made sense after putting it all together and looking at the entire end to end offer experience. My product team and I broke down this multi-phase project into testable outputs that could be achieved over several sprints. The team managed to complete building out the trigger (pink bar) and the vertical drawer late last year, achieveing half of the planned improvements. When I switched over to a different team in the new year, I handed over the project to a couple of other designers who are currently making headway into creating the brand tab functionality.
